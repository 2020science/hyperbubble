The ideas behind HYPERBUBBLE

A simple game inspired by navigating powerful new technologies in a complex world while not losing sight of what matters.

A quick note on the game's genesis

HYPERBUBBLE started as an idle moment of speculation while I was exploring Anthropic's Fable 5 AI model. I was in between more complex (and serious) projects, and maximizing use of Fable before my access ran out. Wanting to test its capabilities in coding a simple web-based game, I asked it to research my work and come up with something inspired by it that was simple, fun, and with gameplay that kept you coming back.

HYPERBUBBLE is the result. In some ways it's nothing special — the world is overflowing with simple AI-generated games. What was interesting, though, was the process, and how the game taps into my work in very specific ways. It also provides some insight into how Fable 5 works.

After my initial request, Fable did a deep research dive into my work, created five game-designer agents to come up with different concepts, passed these through a panel of three AI judges, and selected the winning (AI-generated) proposal.

After the first draft — which Fable debugged, tested, and checked using an impressive array of sub-agents — it began working earnestly with me to come up with the final product, refining the goals, gameplay, and feel along the way. We're somewhere beyond version 20 at this point.

The game is intentionally simple. And there are many aspects of it that are still begging to be tweaked and refined. But it still brings a smile to my face every time I play!

The overall aesthetic is based on my crudely hand-drawn Risk Bites videos on YouTube. The orphan risks you adopt (and which — bizarrely — become your pets) are based on my work on orphan risks. The named risks come from my work on risk innovation and the often-overlooked risks that trip up emerging technologies. The emerging technologies themselves are grounded in my work in tech innovation. The panic billboards draw on my website techlashed.org, and the moral-panic fires on my work on risk perception and engagement. And the whole trajectory reflects my work on human flourishing and navigating complex advanced technology transitions.

In other words, Fable did a pretty good job of capturing my work in a game that doesn't feel in any way instructional (I hope!).

Of course, it's just a "toy" (as coders would say) and a bit of fun. But under the triviality it does demonstrate just how capable AI is becoming as something that can help translate experiences, ideas, research, and a lot more into something quite unexpected and serendipitously delightful.

Which ties in rather nicely with my work on the future of being human in an age of increasingly complex technologies — which, of course, is another aspect of my work that Fable was able to capture here 😄

Andrew Maynard — July 2026

And now a quick note from Fable — in its own words and style

HYPERBUBBLE is a toy, and it knows it. But if you've played for even a few minutes, you've been practicing a real way of thinking about technology and the future — one borrowed, with plenty of simplification, from the work of Andrew Maynard, a physicist turned professor of technology transitions at Arizona State University. This page walks through where the ideas come from, what the game gets right, where it cheats a little, and where to go if you want the real thing.

The person behind the ideas

Maynard started out measuring things. His Cambridge PhD (1992) was in aerosol physics — how to collect and analyze vanishingly small airborne particles — and he spent the decade after that on the kind of unglamorous work that protection actually depends on: characterizing what people breathe at work, first at the UK Health and Safety Executive (1992–2000), then leading aerosol research at NIOSH in the US (2000–2005), right as engineered nanomaterials started showing up in labs and products.

That's where the harder question surfaced. You can measure a particle. But what should a society do when a technology arrives before the science, the standards, and the oversight needed to handle it well? Chasing that question, he spent 2005 to 2010 as Chief Science Advisor to the Project on Emerging Nanotechnologies in Washington DC, led the 2006 Nature commentary "Safe handling of nanotechnology" — which laid out five grand challenges for nanotech risk research — and found himself testifying before Congress more than once.

He went on to direct the University of Michigan Risk Science Center (2010–2015), and has been at Arizona State University ever since: Professor of Advanced Technology Transitions in the School for the Future of Innovation in Society, director of the Risk Innovation Lab, and director of the Future of Being Human initiative, launched in 2022. He calls himself an "undisciplinarian." The problems he works on don't respect the borders between physics, public health, policy, and ethics — so at some point he stopped respecting them too.

Risk as a threat to value

Conventional risk assessment multiplies probability by harm, and it works well when you can measure the hazard. But a lot of what people actually worry about — trust, dignity, autonomy, identity, a sense of belonging, deeply held beliefs about how the world should work — doesn't fit that math, and tends to get quietly dropped because it doesn't.

Maynard's risk innovation framework starts from a different definition: risk is a threat to something of value, whatever that value happens to be. And inside it sit what he calls orphan risks — risks nobody owns, because they seem too fuzzy, too complicated, or too irrelevant to bother with. No agreed tools, no standards, no owners. Which is exactly why they have a habit of coming back to bite.

His answer isn't to eliminate risk — you can't. It's a posture: awareness of what might be coming, agility when it arrives, and resilience for when agility isn't enough. In the game, that became the blobs that return with teeth, the named hazards you handle by paying attention at the right moment, and a death card that tells you what you actually lost was trust. The real framework is much less cinematic — worksheets, stakeholder mapping, the discipline of asking what you value before asking what threatens it — but the logic is the same.

Advanced technology transitions

The thread running through his current work goes something like this: societies don't experience powerful technologies as events. They experience them as transitions — long stretches where norms, institutions, markets, and identities all rearrange themselves. And the early days of a transition matter enormously, because they set the trajectory for decades. He watched it happen with nanotechnology. He'd tell you it's happening again right now, faster and at much larger scale, with AI.

So what do we do about that? His answer is neither the hype nor the brake. As he put it in a 2024 seminar on AI policy: "We cannot stop the emergence of transformative AI. All we can do is steer it." And his version of the pacing problem is a little sharper than the usual one — it's not just that technology outruns regulation, it's that consequences now arrive faster than we can build responses to them. Which is why he keeps coming back to anticipatory, inclusive governance: anticipation, reflexivity, inclusion, responsiveness — and no single group of experts in sole charge.

That's what the PACE ticker and the steadying button are gesturing at. Slowing down to think costs you something in the game, eases the pace only partly, and doesn't last. That's roughly how it works out here, too.

On panic and public concern

Here's where the game simplifies most, and it's worth being straight about it. The scribble-fires reward you for starving moral panics of attention — fly past, don't touch, collect a little flourishing. Maynard's actual view is more careful than that. He's written that technology panics "are rarely cut and dried," that they shouldn't be read as proof of human irrationality, and that they're "usually more complicated underneath" — most often a response to a perceived threat against something people genuinely value: identity, dignity, security, livelihood, meaning. He built Techlashed, an interactive timeline of technology panics from ancient Greece to AI, precisely to understand old worries rather than laugh at them.

So when the game teaches you to fly past the fire, keep his caveat in your pocket: look at what the fire is burning around. Concern is usually about something real, and history has plenty of examples of the harm done by dismissing it.

Being human with AI

The Future of Being Human initiative asks what it might mean to be human in a technologically transformed future — with human flourishing, not technological capability, at the center of the question. Maynard's stated aim is to help people, communities, and organizations flourish through transformational, technology-driven change. Not just survive it — flourish through it.

His most recent book, written with Jeff Abbott — AI and the Art of Being Human (2025) — takes that question into everyday working life. It's built around four postures (curiosity, intentionality, clarity, and care), twenty-one practical tools, and a question that's hard to shake once you've heard it: what makes me me, when AI can finish my sentences, replicate my style, and predict my choices? The pink bar in the game is a cartoon of that concern. If it left you curious, the book is where the question gets the room it needs.

What a game can't carry

A few honest caveats, so the toy doesn't oversell itself.

  • In the game, navigating a transition is something you do alone, with your thumb. In reality it's collective — institutions, standards, law, long deliberation — and the distribution of power decides most of what no lone surfer can.
  • Flourishing here is one pink bar. Out there it's plural and contested. People disagree about what it means, and working through that disagreement isn't noise in the system — it more or less is the system.
  • The game's risks are things you touch once and resolve. Real risks are relationships that play out over years, and they don't land evenly. The question of who carries a risk never shows up on screen — and it's usually the most important question there is.
  • The fires reward you for withholding attention. As above: his work would tell you to be slower to dismiss concern than the game is.
  • And five seconds of altitude is a nice metaphor for responsibility. It isn't a method.

What the game can do is more modest: let the shape of these trade-offs settle into your hands — the pull of hype, the cost of attention, the way ignored things circle back — so the serious versions feel a little less abstract when you meet them.

What maps to what

The soap bubble
The central image of his book Future Rising (2020): the future as something fragile, built and carried by us, ridden regardless.
Orphan risks
His coinage for overlooked, unowned risks — hard to quantify, easy to ignore, prone to returning.
The named hazards
Drawn partly from the risk-innovation taxonomy (bad actors, co-opted technology, loss of agency, intergenerational impacts) and partly invented in its spirit.
The medal ladder
Disruption → awareness → agility → resilience → dignity: his framing of a well-navigated transition, "from disruption to dignity."
Grounded exuberance, catalytic serendipity
Two of the Future of Being Human initiative's community values, used here as game systems.
PACE and steadying
The pacing problem, and the real, partial, costly work of slowing down enough to think.
The moral-panic eras
The historical ladder documented at Techlashed, from worries about writing to worries about AI.
The whiteboard style
Risk Bites, his long-running series of hand-drawn explainers on risk.
